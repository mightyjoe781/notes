# Behavioral Interviews

* purpose : objectively measure a potential employee's past behaviors as a predictor of future results
* format : candidates are asked to give specific examples of when they demonstrated **particular behavior or skills**

Example Questions

- Tell me about a time when you worked as a part of team to successfully execute a projects
- Tell me about a time you failed
- What is a project you are most proud of ?

A lot of above questions are open-ended and could be answered as yes/no in a normal conversation, but in the interview its better to practice a "yes, and " mentality. Provide Context for your interviewer with an example that can help you demonstrate depth of **skills and knowledge**

**Tips**

1. Focus your responses on **actual** behaviors and emotions
2. Describe your role in past situations
3. Provide *specific* examples of your actions
4. Reveal your skills *related* to the job

Based on LinkedIn survey from hiring managers, following are the most important skills,

* Adaptability
* Culture Add
* Collaboration
* Leadership
* Growth Potential
* Prioritization

## STAR Method

Effective formula for structuring your behavioral interview response.

* **Situation** (20%), explain the situation so that your interviewer understands the context of your example, they do not need to know every detail!
- **Task** (10%), talk about the task that you took responsibility for completing or the goal of your efforts.
- **Action** (60%), describe the actions that you personally took to complete the task or reach the end goal. Highlight skills or character traits addressed in the question.
- **Result** (10%), explain the positive outcomes or results generated by your actions or efforts. Here, it is important to highlight quantifiable results. You may also want to emphasize what you learned from the experience or your key takeaways.

### Sample Response - Kinesis-Databricks Streaming Platform

- **Situation** : At FirmA, our data platform team had a critical reliability gap. Around 300 upstream producer services were pushing trade and market data into AWS Kinesis, but the downstream ingestion into our data lake had no formal SLA - data could be anywhere from 10 minutes to over an hour stale, and failures were silent. The team had been living with this for over a year.
- **Task**: I was given end-to-end ownership to design and deliver a streaming pipeline that could ingest 50+ GB per day with a guaranteed 2-minute SLA - without dropping messages or introducing duplicate records downstream.
- **Action** : I broke the problem into three layers.  
    1. **First, the ingestion layer.** With 300 concurrent producers, hot partitioning was a real risk - some symbols traded orders of magnitude more frequently than others. I designed a composite shard key using a hash of the instrument ID plus a time-bucket suffix, which distributed load evenly without requiring producers to know about each other. I validated the distribution using CloudWatch shard-level metrics before sign-off.  
    2. **Second, the transformation layer.** I built PySpark Structured Streaming jobs on Databricks consuming from Kinesis in micro-batches. The tricky part was schema evolution - upstream teams pushed schema changes without coordinating with us. I introduced schema registry integration with a soft-merge strategy: new nullable fields were absorbed automatically, breaking changes triggered alerts and graceful degradation rather than crashes. I also drove design reviews on partition pruning and write amplification, because naive appends to Delta tables were degrading read performance over time.  
    3. **Third, correctness.** Since Kinesis gives at-least-once delivery, I implemented idempotent consumers using checkpoint offsets persisted in a Delta table, making replay safe without double-counting. For financial data, a duplicate trade record is a compliance issue, not just a data quality one.
* **Result**: We hit the 2-minute SLA from day one and sustained it at 50+ MB/s aggregate throughput. The idempotency design resulted in zero duplicate records across the first three months of production. Beyond the numbers, it changed how the team worked - we went from ad-hoc ingestion to a structured design review process for every new pipeline. I now use this as the reference architecture when mentoring junior engineers.

### Sample Response - Centralized policy evaluation platform

- **Situation** : At FirmA, data access is tightly regulated - whether a user can see a financial figure, a geographic region's data, or a node in an org/legal-entity hierarchy depends on a combination of their identity, their role, and the firm's compliance policies. This logic was scattered across individual services: each team was re-implementing their own entitlement checks, with inconsistent rules and no central audit trail. As the number of internal data consumers grew, this became a reliability and compliance risk.
- **Task**: I designed and built a centralized policy evaluation service - a single authoritative system that any downstream consumer could call to determine whether a user was permitted to view or interact with a given piece of data. The service needed to handle three entitlement dimensions: financial data visibility, geographic data access, and hierarchical data scoping (org trees and legal entity structures). It also had to be a Python service capable of sustaining 100,000 requests per second in production.
- **Actions**
    1. Policy model design. I designed a rule authoring model that let compliance and product owners express policies declaratively - what financial data a user can see, which geographic regions, and how deep into an org hierarchy they have access. The hierarchy dimension was particularly interesting: a user granted access to a parent node implicitly gets access to all descendants, so I designed the evaluation to work against flattened level structures (lvl_1...lvl_N) for efficient SQL-compatible lookups without recursive traversal at query time.

    2. LDAP-backed identity resolution. Rather than asking each caller to pass enriched user context, the service resolved identity from LDAP - determining which policies applied to a given user based on their group memberships and attributes. This made the API surface clean: callers pass a user ID and a resource descriptor; the service does the rest.

    3. Scaling a Python service to 100k req/s. Python's GIL and default synchronous execution don't naturally get you there. I identified the critical bottlenecks: LDAP resolution was high-latency and repeated for the same users, and policy evaluation was CPU-bound for complex intersection rules. I tackled this with aggressive multi-layer caching - user -> policy mappings were cached with appropriate TTLs so LDAP was only hit on cold paths. I ran the service with multiple async workers behind a load balancer, tuned connection pools, and profiled hot paths to remove serialization overhead. I also kept the evaluation logic stateless so horizontal scaling was straightforward.
    4. Consumer contract and governance. Since this was a shared platform service with many callers, I defined a strict API contract and versioning policy. I also ensured the service emitted structured audit logs on every evaluation - which user, which policy was applied, which access was granted or denied - which was a compliance requirement. I worked across teams to migrate existing ad-hoc entitlement checks onto the new service.
- **Outcomes** : The service became the single source of truth for data entitlement evaluation across multiple internal consumers. Consolidating the logic eliminated inconsistent policy enforcement across teams, and the audit trail satisfied compliance requirements that previously had no centralized answer. The key engineering lesson was that the hardest part of building a shared platform service isn't the logic - it's the contract: once many teams depend on you, every API decision is load-bearing.
